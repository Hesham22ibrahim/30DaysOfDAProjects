Want to understand how profitable your food delivery service is? Unveiling the Profitability of Food Delivery:¶

To understand how much money your food delivery service makes, we need to examine all its expenses. This includes direct costs like delivery fees and packaging, as well as indirect costs like customer discounts and restaurant commissions. By comparing these costs to the revenue earned (mainly from order value and commission fees), we can determine the profitability of each order.

Here's how we can conduct a Food Delivery Cost and Profitability Analysis:

  1. Data Collection: We begin by gathering detailed information about every aspect of your food delivery operations.
  2. Data Cleaning: This data might have inconsistencies, missing entries, or irrelevant details. We'll clean it up to ensure accuracy.
  3. Feature Engineering: We'll identify specific factors within the data that can impact your costs and profits.
  4. Cost Breakdown: We'll categorize all the costs associated with each order. This includes fixed costs (like packaging) and variable costs (like delivery fees and discounts).
  5. Revenue Analysis: We'll determine the revenue generated from each order, focusing on commission fees and the order value before any discounts are applied.
  6. Profitability Assessment: For each order, we'll calculate the profit by subtracting the total costs from the revenue. Analyzing this data will reveal trends in profitability across all orders.
  7. Strategic Recommendations: Based on the analysis, we'll develop strategies to improve your profitability.
  8. Financial Impact Simulation: We'll use the data to predict the financial outcome of proposed changes, such as adjusting discount or commission rates.

from datetime import datetime

# convert date and time columns to datetime
food_orders['Order Date and Time'] = pd.to_datetime(food_orders['Order Date and Time'])
food_orders['Delivery Date and Time'] = pd.to_datetime(food_orders['Delivery Date and Time'])

# first, use re to extract numeric values from the 'Discounts and Offers' string
food_orders['Discount Percentage'] = food_orders['Discounts and Offers'].str.extract(r'(\d+)').astype('float')

# for percentage discounts, calculate the discount amount based on the order value
food_orders['Discount Amount'] = food_orders.apply(lambda x: (x['Order Value'] * x['Discount Percentage'] / 100) 
                                                   if x['Discount Percentage'] > 1 
                                                   else x['Discount Percentage'], axis=1)

# adjust 'Discount Amount' for fixed discounts directly specified in the 'Discounts and Offers' column
food_orders['Discount Amount'] = food_orders.apply(lambda x: x['Discount Amount'] if x['Discount Percentage'] <= 1
                                                   else x['Order Value'] * x['Discount Percentage'] / 100, axis=1)

Cost and Profitability Analysis¶

To uncover how much money we make on each order, we'll factor in all the associated costs. Here's what we'll include:

  • Delivery Fees: The fees we charge to get food from restaurants to customers.
  • Payment Processing Fees: The fees for handling customer payments.
  • Discount Amounts: Any discounts offered to customers on their orders. By totaling these costs for each order, we can gain a clear picture of our overall cost structure.

On the flip side, our revenue comes primarily from commission fees paid by restaurants. To determine our net profit, we'll simply subtract the total costs (including discounts) from this commission revenue.

Now that we understand the cost and revenue components, let's dive into the analysis!

In [4]:
# calculate total costs and revenue per order
food_orders['Total Costs'] = food_orders['Delivery Fee'] + food_orders['Payment Processing Fee'] + food_orders['Discount Amount']
food_orders['Revenue'] = food_orders['Commission Fee']
food_orders['Profit'] = food_orders['Revenue'] - food_orders['Total Costs']

# aggregate data to get overall metrics
total_orders = food_orders.shape[0]
total_revenue = food_orders['Revenue'].sum()
total_costs = food_orders['Total Costs'].sum()
total_profit = food_orders['Profit'].sum()

overall_metrics = {

Ouch! We're Running at a Loss¶

The analysis reveals some tough news: our overall expenses for food delivery are higher than the revenue generated from commission fees. This means we're currently experiencing a net loss. This suggests our current pricing strategy, including commission rates, delivery fees, and discounts, might not be sustainable for long-term profitability.

Turning the Tide: Strategies for Profitability¶

Our analysis has revealed a key culprit behind our losses: discounts on food orders. To steer towards profitability, we need a new game plan for discounts and commissions.

The ideal scenario is finding a sweet spot – a balance where we can offer attractive discounts without sacrificing profits. To achieve this, let's delve deeper into the characteristics of profitable orders. Specifically, we're looking for:

  • A new average commission percentage based on profitable orders.
  • A new average discount percentage for profitable orders, that could serve as a guideline for what level of discount still allows for profitability.

By identifying these averages, we can develop adjustments that not only make individual orders profitable but also improve our overall profitability across the board.

In [11]:
# filter the dataset for profitable orders
profitable_orders = food_orders[food_orders['Profit'] > 0]

# calculate the average commission percentage for profitable orders
profitable_orders['Commission Percentage'] = (profitable_orders['Commission Fee'] / profitable_orders['Order Value']) * 100

# calculate the average discount percentage for profitable orders
profitable_orders['Effective Discount Percentage'] = (profitable_orders['Discount Amount'] / profitable_orders['Order Value']) * 100

# calculate the new averages
new_avg_commission_percentage = profitable_orders['Commission Percentage'].mean()
new_avg_discount_percentage = profitable_orders['Effective Discount Percentage'].mean()

recommended_commission_percentage = 30.0  # 30%

                                        food_orders['Simulated Discount Amount'])
